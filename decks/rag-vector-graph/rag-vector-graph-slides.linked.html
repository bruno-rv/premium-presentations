Data & AI Architecture

RAG, Vector
& Graph Databases

How retrieval, similarity search, and relationships compose modern AI systems

Set the whole-stack promise: retrieval is not one technology. It is a composition of grounding, similarity, and structure.

Set this up as a layered architecture. RAG answers “where does the model get facts?”, vectors answer “how do we find similar material?”, and graphs answer “how do facts connect?”

“
Models memorize patterns — they do not know your data. RAG grounds answers; vectors find meaning; graphs preserve structure.
Grounding beats guessing

This is the core thesis. Models are fluent, but they are not connected to current private data unless retrieval brings that data into context.

A concrete example helps: the model may know what a refund policy is in general, but it does not know your current refund policy unless retrieval brings that document into the prompt.

Roadmap

One stack, three lenses

Use the roadmap to prevent confusion. RAG is the pattern, vectors are one retrieval mechanism, and graphs are the relationship layer.

The three lenses are not competitors. In many systems they appear in sequence: retrieve candidate text, enrich or constrain it with structure, then generate an answer with citations.

01

Act 1

Retrieval-Augmented
Generation

Ground the model in your corpus — not parametric memory alone.

Start Act 1 with the job of RAG: reduce guessing by grounding answers in a corpus the organization controls.

Make the promise modest and credible. RAG does not make models perfect; it gives them a better working memory for the question currently being asked.

Definition

What is RAG?

Retrieval-Augmented Generation combines a language model with an external knowledge source. At query time, relevant passages are retrieved and injected into the prompt — so the model answers from your corpus, not only training weights.

Why it matters

Reduces hallucination on domain facts, supports private data, and lets you update knowledge without retraining.

Core loop

  • User question
  • Retrieve top-k chunks
  • Build grounded prompt
  • LLM generates answer
  • Cite sources (optional)

Keep the definition operational: retrieve, inject, generate. The value is not just lower hallucination; it is keeping answers tied to source material.

Stress the time-of-query nature. Instead of retraining every time a policy changes, we update the corpus and let retrieval bring the latest relevant passages into context.

Pipeline

Offline index + online query

flowchart TB
  subgraph offline [Offline build index]
    D[Documents] --> C[Chunk]
    C --> E[Embed]
    E --> I[(Vector index)]
  end
  subgraph online [Online answer user]
    Q[User query] --> EQ[Embed query]
    EQ --> R[Retrieve top-k]
    I --> R
    R --> P[Prompt + context]
    P --> L[LLM]
    L --> A[Answer]
  end

Walk offline versus online. Offline does the expensive preparation; online has to be fast enough to serve the user.

Offline indexing is where you decide chunk size, metadata, embeddings, and refresh cadence. Online query is where those decisions are tested under latency and quality pressure.

Reality check

Where RAG fails

Most production pain is retrieval quality — not the LLM.

Bad chunks

Wrong boundaries lose context across paragraphs

Stale index

Docs updated but embeddings not refreshed

Weak recall

Right answer exists but never retrieved

Fix order

Measure recall@k and nDCG on a golden set before swapping models or adding agents.

This is where teams usually learn the hard lesson. If the right evidence never reaches the prompt, changing models will not fix the answer.

Give teams permission to debug retrieval first. Before changing prompts or models, inspect the retrieved chunks and ask whether a knowledgeable human could answer from that evidence.

02

Act 2

Vector
Databases

Similarity search at scale — meaning as geometry.

Now zoom into the retrieval engine most teams start with. Vectors turn similarity into an indexed lookup.

Vectors are usually the first practical retrieval layer because they handle natural language mismatch. They are the “good enough semantic front door” for many RAG systems.

Embeddings

Meaning as geometry

What gets stored

Text passes through an embedding model → fixed-size float vectors (768–3072 dims). Similar meaning → vectors close in space.

  • Cosine similarity (most common)
  • Euclidean / dot product
  • Metadata filters (tenant, date, ACL)
# conceptual similarity
embed("refund policy EU")
  → [0.02, -0.14, …]

embed("return rules Europe")
  → [0.03, -0.13, …]
cosine ≈ 0.91

embed("kubernetes ingress")
  → [-0.31, 0.08, …]
cosine ≈ 0.12

Make embeddings concrete: a sentence becomes coordinates. The readable text stays in storage, but the search key is geometric.

The readable object is still the passage. The embedding is just the coordinate that lets us find passages that mean similar things even when the words differ.

Search

Approximate nearest neighbor (ANN)

Exact brute-force search does not scale — indexes trade recall for speed.

flowchart LR
  Q[Query vector] --> H[HNSW / IVF index]
  H --> K[Top-k neighbors]
  K --> F[Metadata filter]
  F --> R[Reranker optional]
  R --> C[Context chunks]

Explain the bargain: ANN gives up perfect recall to get interactive speed. Good systems measure that trade instead of hoping.

The operational question is how much recall you can trade for speed. Production teams should measure missed relevant chunks, not just benchmark latency.

03

Act 3

Graph
Databases

When the answer lives in the path, not the paragraph.

Shift from similar passages to connected entities. Graphs matter when the answer depends on relationships the text alone does not expose.

Introduce graphs as the missing relationship layer. If the answer needs supplier relationships, document citations, ownership, or lineage, plain passage similarity may not be enough.

Structure

Graphs model relationships

Nodes are entities (Person, Product, Document). Edges are typed relationships (WORKS_AT, CITES, OWNS). Queries traverse paths — ideal when the answer depends on who connects to whom.

Example

“Which suppliers of our Tier-1 vendor also export to region X?” — a 3-hop graph query, not a single embedding match.

Use the examples to make the model feel physical: entities are nodes, relationships are typed edges, and a query is a walk through that structure.

A graph lets you ask questions like “what connects these two things?” or “what is downstream of this change?” Those are hard to answer with chunks alone.

Decision

Vector vs graph — when to use

Semantic recall

Vector database

Unstructured text, FAQs, tickets — “passages like this question.”

Scale: millions of chunks + ANN indexes.

vs
Structured paths

Graph database

Entities, lineage, fraud — multi-hop reasoning & GraphRAG summaries.

Use when relationships are the product.

This slide is the decision point. If the user asks for a passage, use vectors; if they ask for a chain or dependency, use graph structure.

Do not present vector versus graph as ideology. Ask what the user expects back: a relevant passage, a connected explanation, or both.

Hybrid

Combine both in production

flowchart TB
  Q[User question] --> V[Vector recall broad semantic]
  Q --> G[Graph expand entities and hops]
  V --> M[Merge + dedupe]
  G --> M
  M --> RR[Rerank — cross-encoder / LLM]
  RR --> P[Grounded prompt]
  P --> LLM[LLM response]

Vectors cast a wide net; graphs enforce structure; reranking picks what belongs in context.

Explain hybrid as a pipeline, not a buzzword. Vectors widen recall, graphs constrain or expand, and reranking decides what deserves context.

A common hybrid pattern is vector search first, graph expansion second. Retrieve candidate entities or documents, follow relationships around them, then rerank the combined evidence.

“
Start with vectors for semantic search. Add graphs when relationships are the product. Measure retrieval before you tune the model.

End with the operating advice: start simple, then add structure when measurement shows vector recall is not enough.

End with measurement: look at failed questions, inspect retrieved context, and only add graph complexity when the failure is clearly structural rather than just poor chunking or embeddings.
